Synthetic Semaphore Data

How we generate unlimited fake training clips - and why it lets us bootstrap a model before collecting much real data.

The one-sentence version. We never record fake video. We compute stick-figure joint coordinates directly from known arm angles, animate between them, then rough them up with realistic noise - producing data in the exact same format MediaPipe gives us from your real camera.

1. Why bother with synthetic data?

The model needs to learn two different things:

  • The temporal machinery - how a stream of body poses turns into letters over time. This is generic; it does not depend on your personal style.
  • Your specific vocabulary - what your particular small, cursive gestures mean. This only your real clips can teach.

Collecting thousands of real clips is slow and tiring. So we pretrain the temporal machinery on cheap, unlimited synthetic data, then fine-tune on your real clips. The synthetic data is a structured stand-in, not perfect semaphore - its job is to give the network a head start, not to be the final truth.

synthetic (unlimited)-> pretrain temporal model-> fine-tune on YOUR clips-> recognize your semaphore

2. The coordinate system

Everything lives in MediaPipe's image space: x and y run from 0 to 1 across the frame, and - importantly - y increases downward (top of frame is 0). Each frame is 33 body landmarks, each with (x, y, z, visibility) = 132 numbers. A clip is a stack of these frames: [frames, 132]

An arm is defined by one angle, measured from straight-down (0°), swinging outward to horizontal (90°) and up (180°):

The math is two lines - a unit direction from the angle, scaled by arm length and added to the shoulder:

def _arm_points(shoulder, side, angle_deg, arm_len, bend=0.0):
    a = np.radians(angle_deg)
    direction = np.array([side * np.sin(a), np.cos(a)])  # y is down
    perp = np.array([-direction[1], direction[0]])
    sx = np.array(shoulder)
    return sx + 0.5*arm_len*direction + bend*perp,  sx + arm_len*direction
    #       elbow (slightly bent)                       wrist

3. Building one frozen pose

A full pose places the fixed body parts (shoulders, nose, hips) and both arms. For a space, the hands instead come home to the belly button - matching how you actually sign a space.

Each letter is just a pair of arm angles. We enumerate distinct angle pairs and assign them to A-Z:

POSITIONS = [-45, 0, 45, 90, 135, 180]
_PAIRS = [(l, r) for l in POSITIONS for r in POSITIONS if not (l==0 and r==0)]
CANON = {ch: _PAIRS[i] for i, ch in enumerate("abc...z")}   # (0,0) reserved for space
Honesty note. This angle table is a structured stand-in, not verified real semaphore. That is fine: it gives the network 26 clearly-different, consistent motion targets to cut its teeth on. Your real clips - and the whole point that your semaphore is personal and compact - define the true meanings during fine-tuning.

4. Turning poses into motion

A word is a path through poses: home -> h -> e -> l -> l -> o -> home. We interpolate between consecutive poses to create in-between frames, using a smoothstep ease so the motion accelerates and decelerates like a real arm (this is the "cursive" blur you described - letters flowing into each other rather than snapping).

for k in range(1, move+1):
    t = k / move
    t = t*t*(3 - 2*t)              # smoothstep easing
    frames.append((1-t)*prev + t*cur)  # blend previous pose -> current pose
frames.extend([cur] * hold)            # pause on the letter for a few frames

The number of in-between frames (move) and the pause length (hold) are randomized per transition, so the same word is signed at different speeds every time. That is how the model learns to be robust to fast vs slow signing.

5. Making it realistic (the hard part)

Clean synthetic data is a trap: the model memorizes it in seconds and learns nothing transferable. So every clip gets roughed up. But there is a subtlety that drives which noise is worth adding.

Key insight. Before the model sees a clip, we normalize it - re-center on the mid-shoulder point and divide by shoulder width. That makes the model blind to where you stand and how big you are in frame. So augmenting global position or global zoom is pointless - normalization erases it. We only add noise that survives normalization.
AugmentationWhat it mimicsSurvives normalize?
Per-pose angle noiseImprecise human signing - the same letter never lands identicallyyes
Rotation about shouldersCamera tilt / body leanyes
Low-frequency driftNatural body sway over the clipyes
Per-joint jitterMediaPipe tracking noise frame to frameyes
Wrist occlusion (freeze)A hand leaving frame, tracking gets stuckyes
Arm length / elbow bend / hip heightDifferent body proportions and your compact styleyes
Global shift / zoomPosition and distance from camerano - skipped
# rotation about the mid-shoulder (camera tilt / lean)
theta = rng.normal(0, np.radians(7))
pts[:] = (pts - center) @ R.T + center

# slow body sway, then per-frame tracking jitter
pts += np.cumsum(rng.normal(0, 0.0015, ...), axis=0)
pts += rng.normal(0, jitter, size=pts.shape)

# ~30% of clips: a hand drops tracking and freezes for a span
if rng.random() < 0.3:
    pts[t0:t1, hand_idx, :] = pts[t0, hand_idx, :]

Measured effect: with clean synth, within-letter spread was ~0 (trivially separable). After this, within-letter spread is about 0.50 while different letters still sit ~0.87 apart - realistic difficulty instead of a memorization shortcut.

6. Where this fits the whole plan

  1. Pretrain on synthetic

    Feed the infinite synthetic stream through normalize -> CNN -> transformer. The network learns to read motion over time. Loss now drops on a healthy curve rather than collapsing instantly.

  2. Fine-tune on your real clips

    Once enough real takes exist per phrase, continue training on them. This is where your actual compact, cursive vocabulary overrides the canonical stand-in. (Right now there are too few real clips to fine-tune - that is what collecting more unlocks.)

  3. Swap to a sequence head (CTC)

    Whole-clip classification cannot represent "h-e-l-l-o is a sequence of letters." A CTC head aligns a variable-length pose stream to a character sequence - the path to open-vocabulary typing and true cursive transcription.

Why sim-to-real looks low right now, and why that is expected. Your semaphore is deliberately personal and non-canonical. A model trained only on the canonical-ish synthetic stand-in should not nail your real signing yet - the synthetic stage only installs the temporal machinery. Real fine-tuning data is the missing ingredient, and it is exactly what you are collecting.
